An RLC circuit consists of an inductor, capacitor, and resistor in series. We explore the response of such an RLC circuit, measuring admittance and phase across a range of driving frequencies with an oscilloscope. We discuss the band pass behavior near resonant frequency and compare our measurement results to theoretical calculations.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
In this lab, we explore the admittance and phase of a series RLC circuit with inductance $L=\qty{22}{\milli\henry}$, resistance $R=\qty{50}{\ohm}$, capacitance $C=\qty{18}{\nano\farad}$, and external voltage $V_0 \approx \qty{3}{\volt}$. The values of $R$, $L$, and $C$ completely determine the physical characteristics of the circuit. RLC circuits are some of the most elementary oscillator circuits and have applications across basic electronics. The circuit (Figure~\ref{fig:circuit}) reaches maximum current response at its resonant frequency $\omega_0$, and quickly drops to $1 / \sqrt{2}$ within $\omega_0\pm\beta$, while power response drops to $P \propto I^2=1/2$. This band-pass behavior is very useful for devices that need to filter particular frequency ranges, like radios. By adjusting $L$, $R$, and $C$, engineers can have full control over the band pass range. This powerful flexibility motivates our exploration of this circuit.

We can model the sinusoidal nature of the applied AC voltage with a complex phasor voltage $V_E=V_{0}\,e^{i\omega t}$. By Kirchoff's Laws, the circuit will obey the following ODE.

$$\ddot{q}+\frac{L}{R}\,\dot{q}+\frac{1}{LC}\,q=\frac{V_{0}}{L}\,e^{i\omega t}$$

Or, expressed in standard driven harmonic oscillator form with damping factor $\beta=\frac{R}{2L}=\qty{1.1}{\kilo\hertz}$ and resonant frequency $\omega_{0}=(LC)^{-1/2}=\qty{50}{\kilo\hertz}$,

$$\ddot{q}+2\beta \,\dot{q}+\omega_{0}^{2}\,q=0$$

\subsection{Modeling Transient Behavior}

The characteristic equation $r^{2}+2\beta\,r+\omega_{0}^{2}=0$ has solutions found with the quadratic formula: $r_{1,2}=\frac{1}{2}(-2\beta\pm \sqrt{4\beta^{2}-4\omega_{0}^{2}})=-\beta\pm \sqrt{\beta^{2}-\omega_{0}^{2}}$. Since $\beta^{2}<\omega_{0}^{2}$, we know $r_{1,2}=-\beta\pm i\sqrt{\omega_{0}^{2}-\beta^{2}}$. This is an underdamped harmonic oscillator with a known transient solution, 

$$
x(t) = e^{-\beta t}(c_{1}\,e^{i\omega_{1}t}+c_{2}\,e^{-i\omega_{1}t})=A\,e^{-\beta t}\cos(\omega_1 t + \phi)$$

How long does it take for this solution to decay? Consider the damping time $t_D$ during which the circuit will decay to $1/e$ of the starting value. Referring to Eq.~\eqref{amp_phase}, we set $\beta\,t_D=1$ so $t_D=1/\beta=\qty{0.89}{\milli\s}$, during which we should see $\omega_1 / (2\pi\beta)=7$ cycles. Due to the miniscule nature of these values, the transient solution is of no use to us during this experiment. The transient solutions decay almost immediately after turning the circuit on. 


\subsection{Modeling Steady State Behavior}


The known steady state solution to the ODE is $q_p(t)=De^{i\omega t}$ where $D=|q|\,e^{i\phi}$, where


\begin{equation}
  \label{amp_phase}
|q| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2-\omega^2)^2+4\beta^2\omega^2}}, \quad \tan\phi = \frac{-2\beta\omega}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}
\end{equation}


Differentiate $q(t)$ with respect to time to find the current $I(t)=i\omega\left\lvert{q}\right\rvert\,e^{i\phi}\,e^{i\omega t}=\omega\left\lvert{q}\right\rvert\,e^{i(\phi+\pi/2)}\,e^{i\omega t}$.




\begin{equation}
  \label{curr_phase}
I(t)=\frac{\omega\,V_{0}  / L}{\sqrt{(\omega_{0}^{2}-\omega ^{2})^{2}+4\beta^{2}\omega ^{2}}}\,e^{i\phi_{I}}\,e^{i\omega t}, \quad \phi_I=\phi + \pi / 2
\end{equation}

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

We assembled Circuit \ref{fig:circuit} and sent a driving sinusoidal voltage $V_E$ from a function generator. We used an oscilloscope to measure the voltage $V_R$ across the resistor. Before discussing our figures, please note that component and oscilloscope tolerances introduce measurement uncertainty. The voltage reading from our oscilloscope, particularly of voltage in $V_E$, were often flickering by $\pm5\%$ during measurement. The noted values of $R$, $L$, and $C$ are also subject to uncertainty.

\subsection{Measuring Admittance}


 Admittance is a common way to characterize how easily AC current flows in a circuit, and is easy to measure experimentally and calculate theoretically. Substitute external voltage $V_{E}=V_{0}\,e^{i\omega t}$ in Eq. \eqref{amp_phase}. By Ohm's Law, $V_{E}=I(t) /Y$ and $V_{R}=I(t)$, where $Y$ is the admittance. Solving for $Y$ and taking the norm, we get two equations for theoretical and experimental admittance respectively. 
 


\begin{equation}
  \label{y_exp}
  \begin{aligned}
    |Y|_{\text{theoretical}}(\omega)&=\frac{\omega  / L}{\sqrt{(\omega_{0}^{2}-\omega ^{2})^{2}+4\beta^{2}\omega ^{2}}}  \\
    |Y|_{\text{experimental}}(\omega)&=\frac{V_{R}}{V_{E}}\frac{1}{R},
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}


 On our oscilloscope, we used the built in measurement tool to find $V_{R}$ and $V_{E}$. The value of $R=\qty{50}{\ohm}$ was already known. Figure~\ref{fig:admittance} shows the measured and theoretical admittance in Eqs. \eqref{y_exp}; raw data is tabulated in the appendix (Table~\ref{tab:data}).

\subsection{Measuring Phase}

We used the cursor functionality on the oscilloscope to measure the change in time $\Delta t$ between the t-intercepts of each input and output. The experimental phase shift $\phi_{I,\text{exp}}$ is then given by $2\pi f\Delta t$. The theoretical phase shift $\phi_{I,\text{th}}$ is given Eq.~\eqref{amp_phase} and Eq.~\eqref{curr_phase}. 


\begin{equation}
  \label{phi_exp}
  \begin{aligned}
  \phi_{I,\text{th}}(\omega) &= \tan^{-1} \left[{\frac{-2\beta\omega}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}}\right] + \frac{\pi}{2} \\
  \phi_{I,\text{exp}}(\omega) &= 2\pi f\Delta t
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Within the band pass, we expect $\phi_I(\omega_0\pm\beta)=\tan^{-1} \left[{\frac{-2\beta(\omega_0\pm\beta)}{\omega_0^2 - (\omega_0\pm\beta)^2}}\right]  + \frac{\pi}{2}\approx\tan^{-1} \left[{\frac{-2\beta\omega_0}{2\omega_0\beta}}\right]  + \frac{\pi}{2}=\pi/4$. Similarly, at the resonant frequency, we should see $\phi_I(\omega_0)=0$. Figure~\ref{fig:phase} shows the measured and theoretical phase closely matching, with expected behavior around the band pass. See the Appendix (Table~\ref{tab:data}) for data.

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:conclusions}

We set up a series RLC circuit and powered it with a variable sinusoidal driving voltage through a function generator. We used an oscilloscope to measure external input voltage and output resistor voltage, using that along with time shift data to calculate experimental phase and admittance. After comparing these measurements to known theoretical equations for admittance and phase, we found a reasonable fit for both. We took note of interesting behavior at the resonant frequency $\omega=\omega_0$, where output voltage matches input voltage in both phase and amplitude, and explored how amplitude fall off within the band pass of $\omega=\omega_0\pm\beta$. Since our experimental data matches our theoretical predictions, we consider this a successful experiment.

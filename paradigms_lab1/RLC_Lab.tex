\begin{abstract}
We explore the frequency response of a series RLC circuit by measuring admittance and phase shift across a range of driving frequencies. The circuit exhibits band-pass behavior, with maximum response at the resonant frequency $\omega_0$ and rapid attenuation outside the band pass region $\omega_0\pm\beta$. By adjusting $L$, $R$, and $C$, engineers can control the band pass range, making RLC circuits useful for frequency filtering in applications such as radio receivers. We use an oscilloscope to send a range of driving frequencies into the circuit, and make experimental measurements for admittance (fraction of voltage out to voltage in) and current phase (shifted from voltage in). Our measurements reasonably matched theoretical predictions, demonstrating the circuit's utility for frequency filtering applications, like radios.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
RLC circuits are elementary oscillator circuits with applications across electronics. We construct Circuit~\ref{fig:circuit} with inductance $L=\qty{22}{\milli\henry}$, resistance $R=\qty{50}{\ohm}$, and capacitance $C=\qty{18}{\nano\farad}$. The circuit reaches maximum current response at its resonant frequency $\omega_0$ and quickly drops to $1 / \sqrt{2}$ within $\omega_0\pm\beta$, while power response drops to $P \propto I^2=1/2$.

\section{Theory}\label{sec:theory}

We can model the sinusoidal nature of the applied AC voltage with a complex phasor voltage in Eq.~\eqref{volt_phasor}

\begin{equation}
  \label{volt_phasor}
V_E=V_{0}\,e^{i\omega t}
\end{equation}


By Kirchhoff's Laws, the circuit will obey the following ODE~\cite{gingrich}.

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:ode}
  \ddot{q}+\frac{L}{R}\,\dot{q}+\frac{1}{LC}\,q=\frac{V_{0}}{L}\,e^{i\omega t}
\end{equation}

Or, expressed in standard driven harmonic oscillator form with damping factor $\beta=\frac{R}{2L}=\qty{1.1}{\kilo\hertz}$ and resonant frequency $\omega_{0}=(LC)^{-1/2}=\qty{50}{\kilo\hertz}$,

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:standard_form}
  \ddot{q}+2\beta \,\dot{q}+\omega_{0}^{2}\,q=0
\end{equation}

\subsection{Modeling Transient Behavior}

The characteristic equation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:standard_form} $r^{2}+2\beta\,r+\omega_{0}^{2}=0$ has solutions found with the quadratic formula: $r_{1,2}=\frac{1}{2}(-2\beta\pm \sqrt{4\beta^{2}-4\omega_{0}^{2}})=-\beta\pm \sqrt{\beta^{2}-\omega_{0}^{2}}$. Since $\beta^{2}<\omega_{0}^{2}$, we know $r_{1,2}=-\beta\pm i\sqrt{\omega_{0}^{2}-\beta^{2}}$. This is an underdamped harmonic oscillator with a known transient solution~\cite{gingrich},

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:transient}
  x(t) = e^{-\beta t}(c_{1}\,e^{i\omega_{1}t}+c_{2}\,e^{-i\omega_{1}t})=A\,e^{-\beta t}\cos(\omega_1 t + \phi)
\end{equation}

The transient solution decays quickly enough for us to ignore it. Consider the damping time $t_D$ during which the circuit will decay to $1/e$ of the starting value. Referring to Eq.~\eqref{amp_phase}, we set $\beta\,t_D=1$ so $t_D=1/\beta=\qty{0.89}{\milli\s}$, during which we should see $\omega_1 / (2\pi\beta)=7$ cycles. The minuscule nature of these values show that the transient solutions decay almost immediately after turning the circuit on. 


\subsection{Modeling Steady State Behavior}


The known steady state solution~\cite{gingrich} to the ODE is $q_p(t)=De^{i\omega t}$ where $D=|q|\,e^{i\phi}$, where


\begin{equation}
  \label{amp_phase}
|q| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2-\omega^2)^2+4\beta^2\omega^2}}, \quad \tan\phi = \frac{-2\beta\omega}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}
\end{equation}


Differentiate $q(t)$ with respect to time to find the current $I(t)=i\omega\left\lvert{q}\right\rvert\,e^{i\phi}\,e^{i\omega t}=\omega\left\lvert{q}\right\rvert\,e^{i(\phi+\pi/2)}\,e^{i\omega t}$.




\begin{equation}
  \label{curr_phase}
I(t)=\frac{\omega\,V_{0}  / L}{\sqrt{(\omega_{0}^{2}-\omega ^{2})^{2}+4\beta^{2}\omega ^{2}}}\,e^{i\phi_{I}}\,e^{i\omega t}, \quad \phi_I=\phi + \pi / 2
\end{equation}

Where by Ohm's law, the voltage across the resistor is given by 

\begin{equation}
  \label{volt_r_phasor}
  V_R(t)=\frac{R\omega\,V_{0}  / L}{\sqrt{(\omega_{0}^{2}-\omega ^{2})^{2}+4\beta^{2}\omega ^{2}}}\,e^{i(\omega t + \phi_I)}
\end{equation}

We now derive theoretical admittance and phase from Eq.~\eqref{curr_phase}. Substitute external voltage $V_{E}=V_{0}\,e^{i\omega t}$ in Eq. \eqref{amp_phase}. By Ohm's Law, $V_{E}=I(t) /Y$ and $V_{R}=I(t)$, where $Y$ is the admittance. Solving for $Y$ and taking the norm, we get theoretical admittance (Eq.~\eqref{y_th})

\begin{equation}
  \label{y_th}
    |Y|_{\text{theoretical}}(\omega)=\frac{\omega  / L}{\sqrt{(\omega_{0}^{2}-\omega ^{2})^{2}+4\beta^{2}\omega ^{2}}}
\end{equation}

The theoretical phase shift $\phi_{I,\text{th}}$ is given by Eq.~\eqref{amp_phase} and Eq.~\eqref{curr_phase}:

\begin{equation}
  \label{phi_th}
  \phi_{I,\text{th}}(\omega) = \tan^{-1} \left[{\frac{-2\beta\omega}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}}\right] + \frac{\pi}{2}
\end{equation}

Within the band pass, we expect $\phi_I(\omega_0\pm\beta)=\tan^{-1} \left[{\frac{-2\beta(\omega_0\pm\beta)}{\omega_0^2 - (\omega_0\pm\beta)^2}}\right]  + \frac{\pi}{2}\approx\tan^{-1} \left[{\frac{-2\beta\omega_0}{2\omega_0\beta}}\right]  + \frac{\pi}{2}=\pi/4$. Similarly, at the resonant frequency, we should see $\phi_I(\omega_0)=0$.

Component and oscilloscope tolerances introduced measurement uncertainty in our measurements. The voltage readings from our oscilloscope, particularly for $V_E$, were often flickering by $\pm5\%$ during measurement. The noted values of $R$, $L$, and $C$ are also subject to uncertainty. We used the built-in peak-to-peak measurement tool on our oscilloscope to find $V_{R}$ and $V_{E}$. The value of $R=\qty{50}{\ohm}$ was already known. Experimental admittance is given by Ohm's Law in Eq.~\eqref{y_exp}.

\begin{equation}
  \label{y_exp}
    |Y|_{\text{exp}}(\omega)=\frac{V_{R}}{V_{E}}\frac{1}{R}
\end{equation}

We used the cursor functionality on the oscilloscope to measure the change in time $\Delta t$ between the t-intercepts of each input and output. For each frequency $f$, the experimental phase shift is given by Eq.~\eqref{phi_exp}.

\begin{equation}
  \label{phi_exp}
  \phi_{I,\text{exp}}(\omega) = 2\pi f\Delta t
\end{equation}

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:conclusions}

We characterized a series RLC circuit with $L=\qty{22}{\milli\henry}$, $R=\qty{50}{\ohm}$, and $C=\qty{18}{\nano\farad}$ by measuring admittance and phase shift across a range of driving frequencies. Our experimental measurements closely matched theoretical predictions for both quantities, with some discrepancies introduced by component tolerances. The circuit exhibited maximum voltage response at the resonant frequency $\omega_0=\qty{50}{\kilo\hertz}$ with rapid damping to $1/\sqrt{2}$ within $\omega_0\pm\beta$ for $\beta=\qty{1.1}{\kilo\hertz}$.

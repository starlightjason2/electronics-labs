\begin{abstract}
  Coax cables transmit voltage waves at high fractions of light speed and are ubiquitous in modern communications. We model such a voltage wave with the wave equation and derive expressions for wave speed and reflection/transmission coefficients.  Sending a pulsed wave through a $\qty{156}{\meter}$ long RG-58 coax cable, we observe the waveform with an oscilloscope and calculate wave speed to be $\qty{1.97e8}{\meter / \s}$, matching the data-sheet value within 1.5\%. To simulate a boundary, we use a splitter with a terminating resistor connected to a second oscilloscope channel and measure the amplitudes of the incident, reflected, and transmitted pulses to determine the reflection and transmission coefficients. We observe the expected behavior across a range of terminating resistances, and find that an underdamped model incorporating cable resistance better accounts for amplitude losses at high terminating impedances. This improves the fit of our model, but slight discrepancies continue to persist.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Coax cables are foundational to modern communications, used for transmitting internet, telephone, and television signals worldwide. A coax cable consists of four layers---a center conductor, a dielectric insulator, an outer conducting shield, and a protective jacket---and can be modeled as a distributed RLC circuit with resistance $R$, inductance $L$, and capacitance $C$ per unit length~\cite{gfgcoax}.

We develop and test a model for wave propagation, reflection, and transmission in such a cable. We set up a $\qty{156}{\meter}$ long RG-58 coax cable driven by a function generator; oscilloscope channels are connected at the near and far ends to measure incident, reflected, and transmitted pulses (Figure~\ref{fig:coax_setup}). In our first experiment we measure wave propagation speed by using oscilloscope cursors to find time elapsed between incident and reflected pulses. In our second experiment we measure reflection and transmission coefficients for a range of terminating resistances and compare to theoretical predictions, including an underdamped correction for cable losses. To find coefficients experimentally, we measure the amplitudes of incident, reflected, and transmitted pulses with oscilloscope cursors.

\section{Theory}\label{sec:theory}

We model a traveling wave $\psi(x,t)$ with the wave equation, where $\Gamma$ is the damping coefficient and wave speed $v$.

\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial t^2}-v^{2}\,\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2}=0
\end{equation}

For travelling waves, the wave equation has a known under-damped solution \eqref{wave}, for amplitude $A$, time frequency $\omega$, wave number $k$, phase $\phi$, and wave speed $v=\omega/k$ \cite{georgi}.

\begin{equation}
  \label{wave}
  \psi(x,t) = A\,e^{-\Gamma x/2v}\cos(\omega t \pm kx + \phi)
\end{equation}

At a boundary between two media, an incident wave $\psi_I$ (amplitude $A$) splits into a reflected wave $\psi_R$ (amplitude $B$) and a transmitted wave $\psi_T$ (amplitude $C$). Continuity of displacement and material velocity at the boundary requires

\begin{equation}
  \label{boundary_cond}
  A+B=C, \quad (A-B)\,k_1 = C\,k_2
\end{equation}

The reflection and transmission coefficients follow from Eq.~\eqref{boundary_cond} and the impedance $z$ of each medium \cite{georgi}:

\begin{equation}
  \label{coeff}
  R\equiv \frac{B}{A}= \frac{z_2-z_1}{z_2 + z_1}, \quad T\equiv \frac{C}{A}= \frac{2z_1}{z_2 + z_1}
\end{equation}

Notice that $T = 1 + R$ for all cases. Neglecting damping and substituting values, our cable with characteristic impedance $z_1=Z_0=\qty{53.5}{\ohm}$ (from the data sheet~\cite{spec}) and terminating resistance $z_2=R_\mathrm{term}$:

\begin{equation}
  \label{th_coeff}
  R_\mathrm{th}= \frac{R_\mathrm{term}-Z_0}{R_\mathrm{term} + Z_0}, \quad T_\mathrm{th}= \frac{2Z_0}{R_\mathrm{term} + Z_0}
\end{equation}

For a more accurate prediction, we can take damping into account. Using $v=1/\sqrt{L_0C_0}$, the damping factor in Eq.~\eqref{wave} yields an amplitude decay $e^{-\Gamma L/2v}$ over cable length $L$. The underdamped correction is valid for $R_0 \ll Z_0$ and accounts for resistive losses that the ideal model neglects.


\begin{equation}
  \label{damp_coeff}
  \frac{\Gamma}{2v}=\frac{R_0C_0 v}{2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Measuring Wave Speed}



To measure wave speed, we connected CH1 at the input, leaving the far end open (no terminating resistor) and launching a pulse of height $\qty{1.86}{\volt}$. We see the outgoing and reflected pulses in Figure~\ref{fig:pic1}. Using oscilloscope cursors, we measured a round-trip time $\Delta t = \qty{1.56}{\micro\s}$; since the pulse travels $2l = \qty{312}{\meter}$ in that interval:

\begin{equation}
  \label{wave_vel}
  v=\frac{2l}{\Delta t}=0.657c=\qty{1.97e8}{\meter\per\second}
\end{equation}



This agrees with the data sheet specification of $0.66c$ to within 1.45\%. Note that this is the phase velocity of the electromagnetic wave, not the drift velocity of the conduction electrons. The electrons' material velocity is far smaller: each electron oscillates only slightly to advance the disturbance to its neighbor, similar to sound propagating through air.

\subsection{Characterizing Reflection and Transmission}




In the second experiment, we attached terminating resistors ranging from $20$ to $\qty{150}{\ohm}$ to the far end and connected CH2 across each resistor (Figure~\ref{fig:coax_setup}). For each value of $R_\mathrm{term}$ we used cursors to read the incident ($V_I$), reflected ($V_R$), and transmitted ($V_T$) pulse heights, giving the measured coefficients $R = V_R/V_I$ and $T = V_T/V_I$ listed in Table~\ref{tab:data}. A photograph of the oscilloscope traces clearly distinguishes incident, reflected, and transmitted waveforms (Figure~\ref{fig:pic2}).

Our undamped model fits well at low to moderate resistances but overestimates amplitudes at high $R_\mathrm{term}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:wave_propagation}). This indicates non-ideal energy loss in the cable. Since the reflected pulse travels twice as long as the transmitted pulse, any energy loss is amplified x2, which explains why the transmission curve fits slightly better.

The cable impedance is read directly from the data by interpolating where $R = 0$, giving $Z_{0,\mathrm{exp}} = \qty{53.8}{\ohm}$ (within 0.6\% of the data sheet value). At higher $R_\mathrm{term}$, measured amplitudes fall systematically below the ideal curves, suggesting an energy-loss mechanism not captured by the undamped model.}
  \label{fig:wave_propagation}

To get a better result, we incorporate the damping factor from Eq.~\eqref{damp_coeff} with $C_0 = \qty{100}{\pico\farad\per\meter}$ and $v = \qty{1.97e8}{\meter\per\second}$. This gives us a damping factor $\Gamma/2v = \qty{3.86e-4}{\per\meter}$. A transmitted pulse traverses the cable once and is attenuated by $e^{-\Gamma L/2v} = 0.94$; a reflected pulse makes the round trip and is attenuated by $e^{-\Gamma L/v} = 0.89$. The cable's resistance per meter (from data sheet~\cite{spec}) gives a total resistance $R_0 L = \qty{0.0392}{\ohm\per\meter} \times \qty{156}{\meter} = \qty{6.1}{\ohm}$, which is small compared to $Z_0 = \qty{53.5}{\ohm}$ and justifies the under-damped solution. The results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:wave_propagation_damping} -- the transmission curve fits very well, but the reflected curve is still a bit off. As previously mentioned, we expect any energy losses to be doubled for the reflected case.

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:conclusions}

We investigated pulse propagation in a \qty{156}{\meter} RG-58 coax cable, modeling a travelling wave using the wave equation and deriving a model for wave speed and reflection/transmission coefficients at a variable resistive boundary. Setting up a $\qty{156}{\meter}$ long RG-58 coax cable with a two-channel oscilloscope, we observed the reflection and transmission of voltage pulses with 6 different terminating resistances and found a wave speed of $\qty{1.97e8}{\meter\per\second}$, within 1.5\% of the data-sheet value of $0.66c$. Measured reflection and transmission coefficients follow predicted trends, and we use our data to calculate an experimental $Z_0 = \qty{53.8}{\ohm}$, within 0.6\% of the data sheet value. Predicted coefficients overshoot for larger $R_\mathrm{term}$, so we offer a more accurate damping model with a 6\% single-pass and 11\% round-trip attenuation factor, calculated from the cable's total resistance ($\qty{6.1}{\ohm}$). The damped model has better agreement with the data, but still has minor discrepancies from inefficiencies in the circuit and component tolerances. The pulse travels twice as long for the reflected case, doubling any energy losses compared to the transmitted case; therefore, the errors are more pronounced for our reflection coefficients. However, our model is still a powerful tool for capturing the basic physics of coax cables, and can be used for impedance-matching transmission and minimizing signal loss from reflection. Our overall characterization of wave behavior in a coax cable was successful and our predictions for characteristic impedance and wave speed were very accurate.

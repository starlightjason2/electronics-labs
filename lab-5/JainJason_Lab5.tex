We set up an open-loop op-amp circuit (Ref.~\ref{ref:lm358}). Table~\ref{tab:part1a} shows the measured voltages. At the critical voltage $V_+=V_\mathrm{in} = \qty{2.05}{\volt}$, the output jumps up from $\qty{0.006}{\volt}$ to $\qty{3.77}{\volt}$, demonstrating comparator behavior. Row 6 captures the moment the op-amp responds near instantaneously. The open-loop gain is then,
$$
  G_0 = \frac{V_\mathrm{out}}{V_+ - V_-} = \frac{3.77}{0.02} \approx 188
$$
where the differential input at turn-on is $V_+ - V_- = 2.05 - 2.03 = \qty{0.02}{\volt}$. This is far below the datasheet open-loop voltage of $\approx\qty{140}{V/mV}$ (Ref.~\ref{ref:lm358}). However, since the potentiometer is dialed by hand, the value for differential voltage can only be so small, constraining the final calculation.


\subsection{Follower}


\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{output/part1b.png}
  \caption{Follower configuration: $v_-$ vs $v_+$ with linear fit.}
  \label{fig:part1b}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:part1b} shows the follower configuration data with a linear fit. This confirms the Golden Rules (Ref.~\ref{ref:lm358}), which tell us that $V_+$ and $V_-$ should always be equal. The fitted slope is $m = 1.003$, only a $0.3\%$ difference from the theoretical value of $m = 1$.


\subsection{Non-inverting}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{output/noninverting_opamp.png}
  \caption{Non-inverting op-amp response at various frequencies and input amplitudes. We see an experimental gain varying from 2.79-2.94, a 2-8\% percent difference from the theoretical $G=3$}
  \label{fig:noninverting_opamp}
\end{figure}


Figure~\ref{fig:noninverting_opamp} shows the non-inverting amplifier response at different frequencies and input amplitudes. The output amplitude scales with the gain set by the resistors, where we know the non-inverting gain is given by Eq~\eqref{fig:noninverting_opamp} where for our circuit, $R_1=\qty{1}{\kilo\ohm}$ and $R_2=\qty{2}{\kilo\ohm}$.


\begin{equation}
  \label{non_invert}
  G=1+ \frac{R_2}{R_1}=3
\end{equation}

For a variety of amplitudes and frequencies, we see an experimental gain close to $3$, as predicted by theory.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{output/part2.png}
  \caption{Diode voltage vs current for a non‑inverting amplifier (left) and basic circuit (right). We see a linear (Ohmic) relationship for our circuit on the left, vs the logarithmic relationship from Lab 1 on the right.}
  \label{fig:part2}
\end{figure}

In Circuit~\ref{fig:circuit}, we linearize an LED with a closed-loop op-amp~\ref{ref:lm358} and a transistor. The non-inverting (+) input is connected to a potentiometer, allowing us to test voltage output at a variety of loads resistances. The potentiometer is connected to a voltage divider, which outputs a constant $\qty{5}{\volt} \times 220 / (220 + 330) = \qty{2}{\volt}$, which means $V_+$ is linearly dependent on the potentiometer up to $\qty{2}{V}$. The inverting input $V_-$ is connected to a point after the diode. The op-amp is following the Golden Rules and trying to keep these two input voltages equal, which forces $V_-$ into a linear relationship with current.

The op-amp would normally output a typical current $\approx\qty{20}{\milli\ampere}$ (Ref.~\ref{ref:lm358}), which is not enough to power our LED. The transistor (2N222) amplifies the op-amp's output current by around $\times130$ (measured in Lab 2), which is enough to light up the LED. In this case, the extra current comes from the base $\qty{+5}{\volt}$ going to the transistor's collector. Since $V_-=V_\mathrm{out}$, the op-amp forces the amplified output into the Ohmic relationship exhibited by the potentiometer. By dialing the potentiometer, we have a good tunable current source for the LED.

\begin{abstract}
        We construct a square wave function generator on a breadboard, using an LM358 op-amp, a capacitor, and a potentiometer; the circuit functions as an RC oscillator circuit converting DC to AC current. We discuss the physics of our circuit and develop a theoretical model for calculating output frequency from the time constant $\tau=RC$. For a capacitor with $C=\qty{100}{\micro\farad}$ and potentiometer at $R=\qty{3.2}{\kilo\ohm}$, we found a half-period of $\qty{0.325}{\s}$ with a stopwatch, matching our model's prediction within 8\%. We tested two more capacitors ($C=\qty{75}{\nano\farad}$ and $C=\qty{9.3}{\micro\farad}$) at the same resistance, plotting the square wave op-amp output and the capacitor charge/discharge cycle on a two-channel oscilloscope. The oscilloscope's measured frequencies agreed with theory to within $5\%$, supporting our model and providing a theoretical foundation for analyzing op-amps.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

Op-amps are powerful and fundamental electrical components used widely in modern technology. To explore the applications of op-amps, we build a simple RC oscillator circuit with an LM358 op-amp, capacitor, and variable resistance potentiometer. This circuit uses positive feedback, so the Golden Rules are not strictly obeyed, but the op-amp still maintains its function as a comparator, continuously charging/discharging the capacitor. By dialing the potentiometer or swapping the capacitor, we can adjust the frequency of the resulting square wave. We aim to derive a theoretical model for the oscillation period in terms of the time constant $\tau=RC$, and verify it experimentally. The resulting circuit has applications across electronics for converting DC to AC current, and can even be used for generating sounds at audible frequencies.


\section{Theory}


The three crucial components of this circuit are the op-amp, capacitor, and voltage divider, and we discuss the equations governing each. An op-amp is a comparator and amplifier, taking two inputs and scaling the differential by a high gain factor. This sets the output $V_\text{out}$ until some maximum saturation voltage $V_S$~\cite{graham}. An ideal op-amp follows the Golden Rules, with zero input current $I_\text{in}=0$ and equal feedback voltages $V_{+}\approx  V_{-}$. We assume an ideal op-amp, although we will show that it takes some time for the voltages to equalize (equal to the half-period of oscillation). This is because of the positive feedback in the circuit, which prevents the Golden Rules from being unilaterally applied.

Next up is the capacitor, a device that stores charge; the equations for charge/discharge voltage given total change $\Delta V$ from starting $V(0)=V_0$ are Eq~\eqref{cap_charge}. The capacitor time constant is defined as $\tau\equiv RC$~\cite{graham}.

\begin{align}
        \label{cap_charge}
        V(t) & =V_0+\Delta V\left(1-e^{-t/\tau}\right) \\
        V(t) & =\Delta V\,e^{-t/\tau}
\end{align}

Finally, given two resistors $R_1, \ R_2$, a voltage divider sets a threshold voltage weighted by each resistance~\cite{graham}.


\begin{equation}
        \label{eq:vdivider}
        \frac{V_{\text{divider}}}{V_\mathrm{in} } =   \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}
\end{equation}

Circuit~\ref{fig:circuit} takes input power $\qty{+5}{\volt}$ and feeds it into a voltage divider (lower left) with $R_1=\qty{220}{\ohm}, \ R_2=\qty{330}{\ohm}$, setting reference voltage (measured at TP, top left).

\begin{equation}
        \label{ref}
        V_\text{ref}=\qty{5}{\volt}\left(\frac{ 220}{550}\right)=\qty{2}{\volt}
\end{equation}

The positive feedback ($V_+$) is stabilized by another voltage divider with $R_1=R_2=\qty{10}{\kilo\ohm}$. Plugging into Eq\eqref{eq:vdivider} for $V_\text{divider}=V_+$ and $V_\text{in}=V_+ + V_\text{ref}$  from Eq~\eqref{ref}:

\begin{equation}
        \label{vplus}
        V_+(t)  =    \frac{ V_\text{ref} + V_\text{out}(t)}{2}
\end{equation}

The interesting physics happens in the negative feedback ($V_-$), connected to a capacitor-resistor circuit with exponential charge/discharge. When the capacitor drops below the critical threshold $V_-<V_+$, the output will immediately create a positive voltage and force a charge cycle. The op-amp will rapidly reach some saturation voltage $V_\text{out}=V_S$; for an ideal op-amp this switch happens instantaneously, so at any given moment $V_\text{out}=0$ at ground (forcing a discharge) or $V_\text{out}=V_S$ at saturation (forcing a charge). Because of the positive feedback loop, we assume $V_+$ likewise switched instantaneously. Substituting the constraints for $V_\text{out}$ into Eq~\eqref{vplus}, we see that $V_+$ is constrained by $V_\text{ref}/2 \leq V_+ \leq (V_\text{ref} + V_S) / 2$. Assuming a saturation voltage of our power output, $\qty{5}{\volt}$, we get $\qty{1}{\volt} \leq V_+ \leq \qty{3.5}{\volt}$.

The capacitor, however, takes time to charge/discharge. When $V_+$ flips to $\qty{1}{\volt}$, the capacitor remains stuck at $\qty{3.5}{\volt}$ and discharges towards ground until $V_-=V_+=\qty{1}{\volt}$; then $V_+$ flips to $\qty{3.5}{\volt}$, but the capacitor is still at $\qty{1}{\volt}$ and starts charging up to saturation until $V_-=V_+=\qty{3.5}{\volt}$. Using Eq~\eqref{cap_charge} with $V_\text{ref}=\qty{2}{\volt}$ can give us the "flipping" time in each case. While discharging, $\Delta V = \qty{3.5}{\volt}$; while charging, $\Delta V = 5-1= \qty{4}{\volt}, \ V_0 = \qty{1}{\volt}$ (from the previous cycle). This gap is why we say the Golden Rules are not strictly followed. Solving for the charge/discharge times,

\begin{align*}
        3.5 & =1+4\left(1-e^{-t_1/\tau}\right) \\
        1   & =3.5\,e^{-t_2/\tau}
\end{align*}

Therefore, $-t_1\tau=\ln(1 - (2.5 / 4))=\ln(3 / 8)$ or $t_1=\tau\ln(8/3)$, and $-t_2\tau=\ln(1 / 3.5) = \ln(2/7)$ or $t_2=\tau\ln(7/2)$. The total period $T=t_1+t_2$ is then given by \eqref{period_th}

\begin{equation}
        \label{period_th}
        T=\tau\left( \ln\left( \frac{8}{3} \right)+\ln\left( \frac{7}{2} \right) \right)\approx 2.2\tau
\end{equation}

\section{Experimental Procedure}

We assembled Circuit~\ref{fig:circuit} on a breadboard (Figure~\ref{fig:breadboard}) and powered it with a \qty{+5}{\volt} DC supply. The potentiometer was set to $R = \qty{3.2}{\kilo\ohm}$ (measured with a multimeter).

\end{figure}



For our first experiment, we tested a capacitor with $C = \qty{100}{\micro\farad}$. We observed that the speaker was beeping in time with the LED as expected, and counted a total 10 LED blinks over $\qty{6.51}{\s}$, yielding an experimental half period $6.51 / 20 = \qty{0.3255 }{s}$. This is within 8\% accuracy of the theoretical prediction from Eq~\eqref{period_th}: $1.1(\qty{3.2}{\kilo\ohm})(\qty{100}{\micro\farad})=\qty{0.351}{\s}$.

We then connected a two-channel oscilloscope to the test point (TP) and op-amp output to observe the waveforms. Figures~\ref{fig:osc_75nF} and~\ref{fig:osc_9uF} show the recorded output for capacitance values at $C=\qty{75}{\nano\farad}$ and $C=\qty{9.3}{\micro\farad}$.

Both measurements give a percent difference of $\approx 5\%$ from theory. The op-amp output is a clean square wave toggling between ground and saturation, while the test point (TP) shows the characteristic exponential charge/discharge curves of the capacitor between the two threshold voltages. To verify the exponential shape, we fit \eqref{cap_charge} to the first period of each probe trace  (Figures~\ref{fig:osc_75nF} and~\ref{fig:osc_9uF}), and found the fitted time constants consistent with the theoretical $\tau=RC$. Increasing the capacitance from \qty{75}{\nano\farad} to \qty{9.3}{\micro\farad} slows the oscillation from $\approx\qty{2}{\kilo\hertz}$ down to $\approx\qty{15}{\hertz}$, consistent with the linear dependence on $\tau=RC$ predicted by our model. We also note that the charge and discharge half-periods are not equal ($t_1=\tau\ln(8/3) \neq t_2=\tau\ln(7/2)$), meaning the duty cycle is not exactly 50\%---this asymmetry is visible in the probe traces and arises from the voltage divider reference not sitting at $V_S/2$. The small discrepancies likely arise from component tolerances (rated vs.\ actual values) and our ideal op-amp assumptions---the real LM358 has a finite switching time and a saturation voltage slightly below \qty{5}{\volt}, both of which shift the period from ideal predictions.


\section{Conclusions}



The RC oscillator's behavior is driven by the op-amp juggling positive and negative feedback in a perpetual oscillation. A voltage divider along the positive feedback path sets switching thresholds at $V_+=\qty{1}{\volt}$ and $\qty{3.5}{\volt}$; the capacitor on the negative feedback loop charges/discharges in pursuit, but the positive feedback immediately switches thresholds when it catches up. We derived the oscillation period $T\approx 2.2\tau$ (Eq~\eqref{period_th}) from the capacitor charge/discharge equations, and verified it across three capacitor values: a stopwatch measurement with $C=\qty{100}{\micro\farad}$ matched within 8\%, and oscilloscope measurements with $C=\qty{75}{\nano\farad}$ and $C=\qty{9.3}{\micro\farad}$ matched within $\approx 5\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:freq}). The theoretical exponential fit on the probe traces (Figures~\ref{fig:osc_75nF},~\ref{fig:osc_9uF}) further confirms the model, and reveals an asymmetric duty cycle ($t_1 \neq t_2$) arising from the off-center voltage divider reference. This simple circuit reliably converts a DC supply into a tunable AC square wave, with applications in function generators and audible-frequency sound design.

\section{Time-dependent analysis of active RL filters}

We measure the time constant of the RL circuit, first calculating the theoretical value, then comparing it to the experimental value. The resistance is $R=\qty{996}{\ohm}$ and the inductance is $L=\qty{23.1}{\milli\henry}$, giving us a theoretical time constant of $\tau = L/R = \qty{23.2}{\micro\second}$. Our experimental value is found by observing the time it takes for the inductor current to reach $1/e$ of its total value while discharging, and $1 - (1/e)$ of its total value while charging.

The low-pass RL filter exhibits integration behavior, charging/discharging over the period of the input square wave. The experimental time constant $\tau=\qty{16.9}{\micro\s}$  (average of the two values in Figure~\ref{fig:low_pass_10kHz}) and theoretical time constant $\tau=\qty{23.2}{\micro\s}$ have a percent difference of $27\%$. This is a much higher percent difference than the RC circuit, which could indicate that inductors have more measurement uncertainty or operation inefficiencies than capacitors.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{output/low_pass_square_wave_10kHz.png}
    \caption{Active RL low-pass filter response to \qty{10}{\kilo\hertz} square wave input, showing integration behavior with exponential charging and discharging.}
    \label{fig:low_pass_10kHz}
\end{figure}

The high-pass RL filter exhibits differentiation behavior, charging/discharging at the transitions of the input square wave. This demonstrates the filter's ability to pass high-frequency components while blocking low-frequency signals. The experimental time constant $\tau=\qty{22}{\micro\s}$  and theoretical time constant $\tau=\qty{23.2}{\micro\s}$ have a percent difference of $5\%$. This is a more reasonable percent difference, suggesting that our data for the high pass filter had better singal integrity.

\end{figure}


\section{Frequency response of RL circuit configurations}


We characterize the frequency response of the active RL high-pass filter by measuring its admittance and phase shift across a range of frequencies from \qty{1}{\hertz} to \qty{1}{\mega\hertz}. Using time constant $\tau=\qty{23.2}{\micro\second}$, the theoretical characteristic angular frequency is $\omega_c=1 / \tau=\qty{43.1}{\kilo\rad\per\s}$, and the characteristic frequency is $f_c = \omega_c / 2\pi =\qty{6.86}{\kilo\hertz}$.

The Bode plot (Figure~\ref{fig:high_pass_bode_phase}, left) shows characteristic high-pass behavior with a cutoff frequency $f_{c,\mathrm{exp}}=\qty{7.5}{\kilo\hertz}$ where the admittance is \qty{-3}{\decibel} below the passband level. Compared to the theoretical $f_{c,\mathrm{th}}=\qty{6.86}{\kilo\hertz}$, this gives a percent difference of 9.3\%.


The phase response (Figure~\ref{fig:high_pass_bode_phase}, right) shows the characteristic \ang{45} phase shift at the cutoff frequency, transitioning from \ang{90} at low frequencies to \ang{0} at high frequencies, with a poor fit at low frequencies. This behavior is expected for inductors. For both plots, the fit is near perfect around the characteristic frequency.




\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{output/high_pass_bode_and_phase.png}
    \caption{Active high-pass filter Bode plot (left) showing admittance vs frequency, and phase plot (right) showing phase shift vs frequency. The cutoff frequency is marked where admittance drops by \qty{-3}{\decibel} and phase shift is \ang{45}.}
    \label{fig:high_pass_bode_phase}
\end{figure}

To extract Bode plot information from a square wave, the period should be roughly $>100\tau$. The square wave's sharp transitions contain harmonics at all odd multiples of the fundamental $f_0 = 1/T$. Choosing $T \approx 100\tau$ places $f_0$ well below the cutoff frequency $f_c = 1/(2\pi\tau)$, ensuring many harmonics span the region around $f_c$ where the filter's roll-off occurs. A shorter period pushes $f_0$ too close to $f_c$, leaving few harmonics in the transition band and producing a sparse frequency sweep.

\section{Fourier series analysis on oscilloscope}

In Problem Set 3, we computed the Fourier Series of a square wave, seen in Eq~\eqref{square}.
\begin{equation}
    \label{square}
    \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{2(1-1(-1)^{n})}{n\pi}\,sin(n\omega t) = \begin{cases}

1, & 0 < t < T / 2 \\

-1, & T / 2 < t < T

\end{cases}
\end{equation}

To test this result experimentally, we sent a \qty{400}{\hertz} square wave input signal into our RL circuit and computed the FFT with our oscilloscope. The FFT shows the fundamental frequency and its harmonics, with higher harmonics progressively attenuated by the low-pass filter. The experimental Fourier coefficients at each odd harmonic match the theoretical values from Eq~\eqref{square} to within $1\%$, confirming the Fourier series prediction.


\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{output/fft_low_pass_400Hz.png}
    \caption{FFT analysis of low-pass filter output with \qty{400}{\hertz} square wave input, with theoretical Fourier Series coefficients ( Eq~\eqref{square}) in red. Only integer harmonics have theoretical values, but they line up perfectly with the experiment. }
    \label{fig:fft_400Hz}
\end{figure}


\section{Fourier transform analysis}

In Problem Set 3, we modeled a pulse wave with a function $f(t)$.
%
\begin{equation}
    \label{pulse}
    f(t) = \begin{cases}
        0, & t < -\dfrac{T}{2} \\[6pt]
        1, & -\dfrac{T}{2} \leq t \leq \dfrac{T}{2} \\[6pt]
        0, & t > \dfrac{T}{2}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
%

We computed the Fourier transform of Eq~\eqref{pulse} to be a $sinc$ function. 

\begin{equation}
    F(\omega) = \frac{\sin(\omega\, T/2)}{\omega\sqrt{2\pi}}
\end{equation}
%
To confirm this result experimentally, we sent a pulse wave into our RL circuit and took an FFT on our oscilloscope. We adjusted the frequency to \qty{200}{\hertz} so that only one pulse was visible on our oscilloscope, so that we can approximate it with Eq~\eqref{pulse}. 


We then easily extracted the Bode plot in LabView without needing to sweep a function generator across many frequencies. Figure~\ref{fig:bode_pulse} shows the result. The experimental cutoff frequency is $f_{c,\mathrm{exp}} = \qty{7.57}{\kilo\hertz}$, compared to the theoretical $f_{c,\mathrm{th}} = \qty{7.96}{\kilo\hertz}$, a percent difference of $4.9\%$. The manual frequency sweep in Section~2 yielded $f_{c,\mathrm{exp}} = \qty{7.5}{\kilo\hertz}$, a $5.8\%$ difference. It makes sense that the manual sweep was less accurate, because of natual human error during measurement. The pulse method is also much faster. The bode plot from LabView does become less accurate at higher frequencies due to FFT resolution limits, but around the characteristic frequency, it produces very accurate values.
